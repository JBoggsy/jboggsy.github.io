James Boggs

I am an AI Research Scientist at the Center for Integrated Cognition, where I work on merging modern computer vision models for object detection, segmentation, and scene reconstruction with the Soar cognitive architecture, a long-standing symbolic framework for intelligent agents. My research sits at the intersection of perception and reasoning: how deep learning-based representations can be harnessed to enrich symbolic cognition and enable agents to build internal world models, ground their knowledge in multimodal experience, and act intelligently over the long term.

I recently completed my Ph.D. in Computer Science and Engineering at the University of Michigan, advised by John Laird in the Soar Lab. My dissertation, *A Cognitive Architectural Approach to Visual-Symbolic Reasoning*, explored how neuro-symbolic integration can expand the perceptual and reasoning capabilities of cognitive architectures. Along the way, I taught foundational AI courses, mentored motivated students, and discovered that teaching is one of the best ways to deepen your own understanding.

I earned a B.S. in Computer Science and a B.A. in Philosophy from Union College in 2018. During my undergrad, I worked in the Tensegrity Lab under Dr. John Rieffel, where I researched learning gaits for tensegrity robots. I also interned at the Naval Research Laboratory in Washington, D.C., where I worked on rebellious autonomous agents.

My path into AI began less conventionally: as a high school student studying Latin, I wanted to build a translator. That curiosity led me to Python, then NLTK, and eventually to the broader field of AI. In college, I combined my interests in computer science and philosophy, researching tensegrity robotics while also writing on the plausibility of consciousness in artificial intelligences. Those early experiences cemented my fascination with intelligence — both natural and artificial — and continue to shape my work today.

My goal is to uncover the principles of intelligence while applying them to real-world problems, advancing both fundamental science and practical applications that benefit humanity. I believe the next frontier in AI lies not in chasing full AGI immediately, but in deepening AI’s understanding of the world through embodied, multimodal grounding and robust internal world models. Thus I believe that LLMs, while incredibly powerful tools, are not sufficient for achieving human-level performance in a wide variety of tasks, particularly in embodied contexts.

Side note: I'm a big fan of em dashes — I have been since I wrote for the student newspaper in undergrad — and I won't allow ChatGPT to make me give them up. Frankly, I think it's indicative of the power of LLMs that they correctly identified the elegance and utility of the em dash, a punctuation cleaner and sharper than parentheses.
